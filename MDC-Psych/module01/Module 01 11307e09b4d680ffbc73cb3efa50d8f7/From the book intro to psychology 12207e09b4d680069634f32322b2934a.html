1.1 What is Psychology? Myths and Misconceptions
  1. Psychology is the scientific study of mind (mental processes) and behaviour.1 The word "psychology" comes from the Greek words psyche, meaning life, and logos, meaning explanation.1
  1. Common Myths and Misconceptions

a. Psychology is just common sense: While psychological findings may seem intuitive, scientific studies reveal that people are not always accurate at predicting outcomes.2 The hindsight bias often leads us to believe we could have predicted events that were actually unpredictable.2

b. Psychology is only about therapy and mental illness: While therapy is a significant aspect of psychology, the field encompasses various areas like research, forensic psychology, health psychology, and more.345

c. All psychologists analyse dreams: Dream analysis is primarily associated with psychodynamic psychology.6 Various other perspectives within psychology may not focus on dreams.789

d. Psychologists can read minds: This is a common misconception. Psychologists study mental processes and behaviour through scientific methods.10

1.2 Methods to Study Behaviour: Observation, Interview, Correlation, and Experimental Method

● Descriptive Research: This type of research observes and records specific behaviours.11 This can include:

○ Naturalistic Observation: Observing behaviour in a natural setting without intervention.

○ Case Studies: In-depth investigations of a single individual or a small group.

○ Surveys: Collecting data through questionnaires or interviews.

● Correlational Research: This design aims to discover relationships between variables and predict future events from current knowledge.11 Correlations can be positive (variables increase or decrease together) or negative (one variable increases as the other decreases).11 It's crucial to remember that correlation does not imply causation.11

● Experimental Method: This method manipulates an independent variable to determine its effect on a dependent variable, establishing cause-and-effect relationships.11 Key features include:

○ Independent Variable: The variable manipulated by the researcher.

○ Dependent Variable: The variable measured to assess the effect of the independent variable.

○ Control Group: A group that does not receive the experimental treatment, serving as a baseline for comparison.

○ Experimental Group: The group receiving the experimental treatment.

○ Random Assignment: Assigning participants to groups randomly to minimise pre-existing differences.

● Interviews: This method gathers data through direct questioning, allowing for in-depth exploration of experiences and perspectives. Interviews can be structured (using a set list of questions) or unstructured (allowing for more open-ended discussion).

Question: Explain the difference between correlational and experimental research.

Answer: Correlational research examines relationships between variables but cannot establish causation. Experimental research manipulates an independent variable to determine its causal effect on a dependent variable.

1.3 Perception: Stimulus and Psychological Factors. Errors in Perception

● Perception: Perception is the process of organising and interpreting sensory information to understand our environment. It is influenced by:

○ Stimulus Factors: The physical characteristics of the stimuli, such as intensity, size, and colour.

○ Psychological Factors: Our expectations, motivations, emotions, and past experiences.

● Errors in Perception:

○ Illusions: These occur when our perception is distorted, leading to inaccurate interpretations of sensory input.

○ Perceptual Set: Our expectations and beliefs can influence how we perceive things.

○ Confirmation Bias: The tendency to seek out and interpret information in a way that confirms our pre-existing beliefs.

○ Halo Effect: A cognitive bias where our overall impression of a person influences how we judge their specific traits.

○ Primacy Effect: The tendency to remember information presented first more vividly.

○ Recency Effect: The tendency to remember information presented last more vividly.

Question: How does the halo effect influence our perceptions of others?

Answer: The halo effect occurs when our general impression of a person biases our judgments of their specific traits. For example, if we perceive someone as attractive, we may also perceive them as intelligent or trustworthy, even without evidence to support those judgments.

1.4 States of Consciousness: Sleep, Dreams, Drugs, Meditation, and Hypnosis

● States of Consciousness: Consciousness encompasses our awareness of ourselves and our environment, varying in levels of alertness and attention.

● Sleep: A recurring state of reduced awareness and responsiveness to external stimuli. It is essential for physical and mental restoration.

○ Sleep Stages: Sleep progresses through distinct stages, including REM (rapid eye movement) sleep, characterized by vivid dreams, and non-REM sleep, divided into four stages of increasing depth.12

● Dreams: Mental experiences during sleep, often vivid and emotionally charged.

○ Dream Theories: Various theories attempt to explain dreams, including:

■ Freud's Psychoanalytic Theory: Dreams as expressions of unconscious wishes and desires.

■ Activation-Synthesis Theory: Dreams as random neural activity interpreted by the brain.

■ Cognitive Theory: Dreams as reflections of waking thoughts and concerns.

● Drugs: Substances that alter consciousness, perception, mood, and behaviour. They can have both positive and negative effects, depending on the substance and its use.

● Meditation: A state of focused attention and deep relaxation, achieved through various techniques. It can reduce stress, enhance well-being, and improve cognitive function.

● Hypnosis: A state of heightened suggestibility, induced through relaxation and focused attention. It can be used for pain management, behaviour modification, and therapeutic purposes.

Question: What are the potential costs of sleep deprivation?

Answer: Sleep deprivation can lead to:

● Cognitive Impairments: Reduced alertness, concentration, memory, and decision-making abilities.12

● Mood Disturbances: Increased irritability, anxiety, and depression.12

● Physical Health Problems: Weakened immune system, increased risk of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes.12

● Accidents: Drowsiness can impair driving and increase the risk of accidents.12
